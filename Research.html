Laura García Montoya

Book Manuscript

My book project uncovers largely overlooked variation in long-term patterns of inequality within Latin America and explains how and why some countries managed to escape inequality traps while others did not. Using a combination of qualitative and quantitative methodologies, I find that at critical moments where the main redistribu- tion institutions are being established, the relative strength of the state, vis-à-vis the economic elite, explains long-term trajectories of economic inequality. Through a com- parative historical analysis of two cases— Colombia, a country with high and persistent inequality, and Perú, a case that shows a trajectory of change—I demonstrate how the relative strength of the state shapes the main redistributive institutions and economic inequality as a result. Existing research relies on cross-national comparisons to estimate the average effects of a set of factors in shaping economic inequality. These methods capture short-term variations of inequality but cannot explain when and why countries escape inequality traps in the long run. My project departs from this traditional approach and uncovers the puzzling persistence of inequality in most Latin American countries. Through in-depth comparative analysis of a small number of cases, I offer a historically grounded account of long-term trajectories of inequality and highlights the overlooked impor- tance of redistributive institutions. In particular, I explore the process through which redistributive institutions are created and transformed. In so doing, my project demonstrates the deeply political nature of economic inequality.
